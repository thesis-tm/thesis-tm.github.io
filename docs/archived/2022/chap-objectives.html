4 OBJECTIVES AND EVALUATION CRITERIA

The overall goal is that the student after completing the course should demonstrate a deeper ability to contribute to the knowledge development within chosen field of research in Textile Management and managerial implications for the textile industry. The degree is preparatory for doctoral studies. Thus the goal is to stimulate students to complete an independent, creative and reflexive thesis with a clear academic imprint.

You find the complete course syllabus on the course webpage:

http://www.hb.se/en/Current-Student/My-studies/Course-and-programme-portal/

4.1 Intended Learning Outcomes (ILOs)

For magister theses the words in () under ILOs are not applicable

Knowledge and Understanding

  • (Critically) demonstrate – describe, explain and discuss – specialized knowledge and understanding of scientific problems, research approaches, and formulated theoretical perspectives pertaining to certain topics that touches the research frontier within the scope of Textile Management program

  • Acquire and demonstrate specialized methodological knowledge applicable to the core areas of the Textile Management program

  • Demonstrate insight into current research and development work in areas related to core topics of the Textile Management program

Like in other courses, the goal for students is to gain new knowledge. As authors you deepen your knowledge on how to problematize, in a number of theoretical areas as well as in methodology. Within the thesis course you have to demonstrate that you gained new knowledge in such a specialized area. The chosen area should be of relevance to the society, business administration and most certainly the textile management program you follow.

Skills and Abilities

  • Demonstrate independent, in-depth (and creative) skills and abilities to integrate and apply previously acquired methodological and theoretical knowledge and analyze, assess and draw inferences on complex phenomena within the field of Textile Management

  • Demonstrate an ability to plan and execute a research project within a predetermined time frame

  • Demonstrate ability in speech and writing to report scientific messages clearly and discuss their conclusions and the knowledge and arguments on which they are based, for different audiences

  • Scrutinize written and oral peer presentations, give and receive feedback in a constructive manner.

Thesis writing is geared towards practicing scientific methods and using theories to analyse and interpret empirical phenomena. Using a certain problem, the student should methodically plan and perform an empirical investigation and analyse and interpret this empirical material using theories relevant within the field of their study.

The phenomenon as such is central. Theory and method are tools to gain knowledge about it. The thesis should be focused. This requires clear statements of problem and purpose as well as the ability to stick to these during the entire thesis.

Independence also involves the process in which authors who do the job, i.e., they are supposed to find a relevant problem, gather and read literature and make critical choices during the thesis process, e.g. regarding which theories and methods to use. The tutor is a coach, not a manager and not a customer.

Thesis writing is about creating knowledge in an organized and scientific manner. This implies a systematic approach, conscious reflection, critical stance and a good sense of project management.

Judgement and Approach

  • Demonstrate an ability to make assessments, taking into account relevant scientific aspects related to textile management and related ethical issues, crucial to society, and also to research and development,

  • Demonstrate awareness of ethical aspects in research and development work,

  • Reflect upon individual knowledge and skills progression and acknowledge such gaps (to contribute to knowledge development).

In comparison to a one-year master thesis, the two-year master thesis should exhibit a higher degree of creativity and originality in problem solving, higher methodological awareness and a deeper theoretical foundation and positioning in relation to previous research in the area.

The students are expected to maintain a strong academic integrity. This implies to behave within the boundaries of academic rules and expectations relating to all types of teaching and examination. The first rule is to consult you tutor when you detect a sensitive ethical aspect in yours (or some others) thesis project. And, copying someone else’s work is a serious offence and will lead to disciplinary action. Further, you also judge your contributions in a joint thesis work thus reflecting upon research integrity.

4.2 Grading

Your thesis will be graded by a group of graders (primary grader and tutor). The final grade and feedback for motivation should be considered as unanimous decision taken jointly by the graders who has visualized your performance at difference stages of the thesis course. There are 5 main basis for grading.

Assignment 1: Progression during thesis course (active part in preparatory seminars).

Assignment 2: Final seminar (oral presentation, opposition and defense).

Assignment 3: The written thesis

Assignment 4: Responsibility through self-declaration of undertaken effort.

Assignment 5: Poster presentation at EXIT.

Note that:

  • It is possible to differentiate the grades for group members if their participation and contribution differs significantly. We will use a peer-evaluation process.

  • If students want to write their thesis on commission of an external party, e.g. a firm or an association, they should have in mind that the thesis is still marked based mainly on its academic relevance and quality. Students should, therefore, make sure that the party providing the assignment is interested in an academic report treating a general problem. If this is not the case, the authors either have to abandon that assignment or write a separate report that satisfies the requirements of the external customer.

4.3 Evaluation Criteria

So what are we looking for?

Below we provide some specific questions that guide us in the grading process. But this cannot be considered exhaustive.

  1. Choice of topic, purpose and research questions:

    • Relevance of the topic and ability to show the relevance (logic of reasoning?)

    • Well-defined problematization?

    • Clarity of the purpose and the research questions?

    • Overall level of ambition?

    • …

  2. Frame of reference:

    • Motivation of choice of theory?

    • Relevance of the frame of reference to the problem and purpose of the thesis?

    • …

  3. Method:

    • Presentation of the chosen research strategy and method?

    • Motivated choice of method in relation to the research questions?

    • Awareness of the method’s limitations and research quality?

    • …

  4. Empirical study:

    • Given the purpose and the method is the empirical study relevant?

    • Does the empirical data provide a foundation for the analyses?

    • Is the empirical data performed according to the method outlined?

    • …

  5. Analysis/interpretation in relation to the purpose and the frame of reference:

    • Is the analysis/interpretation related to the purpose?

    • Is the research frontier (represented by the frame of reference) used in the analysis/interpretation?

    • …

  6. Conclusions:

    • Are the conclusions related to the purpose?

    • Are the conclusions related to the research frontier?

    • Are the conclusions reasonable given the empirical study?

    • Are the conclusions trustworthy?

    • …

  7. Originality and creativity

  8. Level of independence

  9. Formalities and quality of language

In addition to these nine questions, there is a tenth one which addresses the authors’ ability to write a thesis that forms a coherent whole. This category is particularly important, and weaknesses in this category can never be redeemed by excellence in other categories. In this way the thesis should form a consistent whole with distinct parts. The authors are advised to point by point compare the above with their own plans during the design stage and with the “almost finished” draft towards the end of the process. Excellence in parts can only marginally make up for shortcomings of the overall impression.

In order to achieve a coherent whole, the authors should also write with the reader in mind. The reader can be assumed to want:

easy access to what the subject matter of the thesis is, and why that is important

  • the thesis to stick to that subject matter, and not make various detours

  • the language to be comprehensible, and that it is easy to understand how the author carried out her study

  • the parts of the thesis to follow a logic order

  • that all parts makes the thesis move forward, towards fulfillment of the purpose

  • to know what is the authors’ own contribution, and what has been borrowed from others

  • to be given the possibility to check for him/herself - in principle - whether or not the conclusions are valid.

On the part of the authors, this demands honesty and an ability to stick to the point, e.g. to phase out material that is not central to the thesis. As an author, one easily becomes blind to one’s own text - it takes some effort to look upon one’s own work with others’ eyes.’
